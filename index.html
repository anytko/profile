Using big data to advance Eco-Evo theory

Utilizing open-source ecological data across hundreds of habitats and thousands of species allows for broad inference in eco-evolutionary dynamics. Currently, rare species are categorized by their range size, habitat specificity, and local population size; however, phylogenetic history and functional traits also play a large part in defining rarity. My work focuses on providing an eco-evolutionary synthesis of rarity to characterize the organismal strategies of species varying in geographic, phylogenetic, and functional trait rarity. I also aim to create software that leverages big data and machine learning to predict patterns of invasiveness, rarity, and ecosystem function.

Ecology & Evolution

Through the creation of 3 axes of rarity (geogrphic, phylogenetic, functional) and the utilization of big data, we have categorized over 5,000 plant species into 1 or 8 organismal strategies. These include:

  • Adaptable Survivor
  • Common
  • Competitively Rare
  • Classically Rare
  • Endemic
  • Indicator
  • Potentially Invasive
  • Relict

These classifications can be used to predict invasiveness, recategorize community function, and challenge what it means do be "rare".

Data Science

The development and deployment of tools used to gather, structure, manipulate, and interpret data is key to advancing science. I've worked to build and establish and R package that utilizes open source databases to make the eco-evo functional categorization of rare species publically available. I plan to continue to develop packages in R and Python that leverage data from sources such as:

  • Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF)
  • TRY Plant Trait Database
  • Forest Inventory and Analysis (FIA)
